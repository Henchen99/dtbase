Stock Forms of Polymers

Polymers are available in a variety of stock forms to suit different applications:

  • Sheet – Large, flat sections of polymer used for signage, packaging, and protective covers.
  • Film – Thin and flexible polymer material used for packaging, wrapping, and waterproofing.

Granules:

  • Granules – Small pellets of polymer, commonly used in injection moulding and extrusion processes.

Rod and Other Extruded Forms:

  • Rod – Solid cylindrical shape used for machining, structural applications, and components.
  • Other extruded forms – Includes tubes, channels, and profiles used in construction, insulation, and automotive applications.

Foam Forms:

  • Foam – Lightweight polymer with air pockets, used in cushioning, insulation, and protective packaging.

Powder Forms:

  • Powder – Fine polymer particles used in coatings, sintering processes, and rotational moulding.

Performance Characteristics of Polymers

  • Toughness – The ability to absorb energy and resist fracture under impact. Tough polymers like ABS are used in protective casings and helmets.
  • Elasticity – The ability to return to its original shape after being stretched or deformed. Elastomers like rubber and TPU are used in flexible seals and footwear.
  • Insulation (Thermal and Electrical) – The ability to resist heat and electrical conductivity. Polymers like PTFE and PVC are used in electrical wiring insulation and heat-resistant coatings.
  • UV Resistance – The ability to withstand degradation from ultraviolet light exposure. UV-resistant polymers like acrylic and polycarbonate are used in outdoor signage and roofing.
  • Ability to be Moulded – The ease with which a polymer can be shaped into a desired form. Thermoplastics like polyethylene and polystyrene are easily moulded for packaging and containers.
  • Resistance to Chemicals and Liquids – The ability to withstand exposure to acids, solvents, and moisture. Polymers like PTFE and HDPE are used in chemical storage tanks and pipes.
  • Melting Points – The temperature at which a polymer transitions from solid to liquid. High-melting-point polymers like PEEK are used in aerospace and high-performance applications.
  • Suitability for Food Packaging Applications – The ability to meet food safety standards and prevent contamination. PET and polypropylene are widely used for food containers and bottles.
  • Biodegradability – The ability of a polymer to decompose naturally over time. Biodegradable plastics like PLA are used in compostable packaging and disposable cutlery.
  • Recyclability – The ability to be reprocessed and reused. Thermoplastics such as PET and HDPE are commonly recycled into new products.
  • Self-Finishing – The ability to be produced with a smooth surface that requires minimal post-processing. Polymers like acrylic and polycarbonate offer high-quality finishes.
  • Ability to be Combined with Other Polymers and/or Additives – The ability to enhance properties through blending or additives. Polymers are often reinforced with glass fibres or stabilisers to improve strength, flexibility, or UV resistance.

General comparisons of Thermoplastics vs Thermosets

Using these general rules will help you answer most questions relating to Polymers:

  • Thermoplastics can be reheated and reshaped where as thermosets only burn with heat, this gives them a wide range of appications
  • Both types of polymer can be recycled but thermoplastics are easier to recycle
  • Thermosetting polymers are heat resistant and more durable, they retain their shape when heated
  • Both types of polymers can be easily pigmented (coloured)
  • Thermoplastics are prone to UV degradation
  • Both polymers are heat and eletrical insulators
  • Both polymers are hygenic, easily cleaned and waterproof

Thermoplastics

High-Density Polyethylene (HDPE):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • High strength-to-density ratio
  • Resistant to impacts and chemicals
  • Stiff and durable
  • Good moisture barrier
  • Less flexible than LDPE
  • Susceptible to stress cracking
  • Difficult to bond (needs welding/adhesives)
  • Limited transparency

Uses: Milk jugs, detergent bottles, pipes, toys, cutting boards


Low-Density Polyethylene (LDPE):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Highly flexible and soft
  • Good chemical resistance
  • Easy to process and seal
  • Transparent in thin films
  • Lower strength than HDPE
  • Poor heat resistance
  • Prone to UV degradation
  • Not as rigid (can deform under load)

Uses: Plastic bags, cling film, squeezable bottles, packaging films


Polypropylene (PP):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Lightweight
  • Hard
  • Impact resistant
  • Chemical resistant
  • Can discolour from UV light
  • Oxidation can cause problems
  • Not heat resistant
  • Difficult to paint

Uses: Medical syringes, carpets, kitchenware



High Impact Polystyrene (HIPS):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • High impact resistance (more durable than regular PS)
  • Easy to thermoform and fabricate
  • Good dimensional stability
  • Accepts paints and glues well
  • Low cost material
  • Poor chemical resistance (attacked by solvents)
  • Brittle at low temperatures
  • Poor UV resistance (yellows outdoors)
  • Flammable (releases toxic fumes when burning)

Uses: Refrigerator liners, food packaging, point-of-purchase displays, protective packaging, toys


Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene (ABS):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Good chemical resistance
  • Hard
  • Tough/durable
  • Heat resistant
  • Naturally good finish
  • Can discolour from UV light
  • Poor solvent resistance

Uses: Mobile phones, safety helmets, pipes


Polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) or Acrylic:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Stiff
  • Hard
  • Durable
  • Good electrical insulator
  • Very brittle
  • Scratches easily
  • Splinters easily
  • Poor fatigue properties

Uses: CD cases, Car light cases, Baths


Nylon:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • High strength and durability
  • Good wear resistance
  • Low friction coefficient
  • Resistant to chemicals and abrasion
  • Absorbs moisture, reducing strength
  • Can degrade under UV light
  • Produces static electricity
  • Difficult to recycle

Uses: Textiles, gears, bearings, ropes, toothbrush bristles, parachutes



Rigid and Flexible Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Good chemical and weather resistance
  • Durable and long-lasting
  • Can be rigid or flexible depending on plasticisers
  • Good electrical insulator
  • Can degrade under UV light
  • Releases toxic fumes when burned
  • Flexible PVC contains plasticisers that may leach out
  • Difficult to recycle

Uses: Rigid PVC: Pipes, window frames, credit cards
Flexible PVC: Cable insulation, inflatable products, flooring



Polyethylene terephthalate (PET):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Good alcohol and oil barrier
  • Weather resistant
  • Can be made transparent
  • Inert/non-toxic
  • Stiff/tough/hard
  • Available in a range of colours
  • Can discolour from UV light
  • Needs to be treated when used for food containers
  • Susceptible to heat degradation

Uses: Fizzy drinks bottles, washing up bottles, fibres for clothing



Thermosetting plastics

Urea formaldehyde (UF):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Strong
  • Hard
  • Heat resistant
  • Good electrical insulator
  • Can emit toxic vapours during manufacture
  • Brittle

Uses: Electrical fittings, handles, control knobs, domestic appliance components



Melamine Formaldehyde (MF):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Hard and durable
  • Excellent heat resistance
  • Good chemical and moisture resistance
  • Scratch and stain resistant
  • Brittle and can shatter under impact
  • Not recyclable
  • Can release formaldehyde during production
  • Limited flexibility

Uses: Kitchenware, laminates, electrical insulation, decorative surfaces, worktops



Polyester resin:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Good electrical insulator
  • Heat resistant
  • Hard
  • Cost effective
  • Short shelf life
  • Emissions are harmful
  • Brittle unless treated

Uses: Glass reinforced boats and cars, garden furniture, castings



Epoxy resin:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Corrosion resistant
  • Electrical insulator
  • Good bond qualities
  • Causes health issues/toxic fumes
  • Brittle

Uses: Adhesives, paints and coatings, electronics
