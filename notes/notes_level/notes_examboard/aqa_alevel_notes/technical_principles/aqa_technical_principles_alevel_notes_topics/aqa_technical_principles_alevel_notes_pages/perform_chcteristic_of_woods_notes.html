Stock forms of woods

Types of Timber and Manufactured Boards and Their Characteristics:

  • Rough Sawn – Timber that has been cut to size but not smoothed or finished. It retains a rough texture and is often used in structural applications or for rustic designs.
  • Planed Square Edge (PSE) – Timber that has been planed on one side to create a smooth surface with a square edge, commonly used in joinery and furniture making.
  • Planed All Round (PAR) – Timber that has been planed on all sides to produce a smooth, finished appearance, making it ideal for furniture and decorative work.
  • Natural Timber – Timber that has not been altered beyond the initial cutting process, retaining its natural colour and grain. It’s often used for high-end furniture and aesthetic applications.
  • Manufactured Boards – Engineered wood products such as plywood, MDF, and chipboard. These are created by binding wood fibres together and are often used in construction and furniture production due to their consistency and strength.
  • Mouldings – Timber that has been shaped or formed into decorative profiles, commonly used for trim, frames, and finishing touches in both traditional and modern designs.

Characteristics of woods

Key Characteristics of Timber and Its Performance:

  • Grain Pattern – The arrangement of fibres in the timber, which can vary in appearance depending on the type of wood. The grain pattern affects the strength and aesthetics of the wood, with straight grain offering higher strength and uniformity.
  • Grain Direction – The orientation of the wood fibres, which can impact the wood’s strength and workability. Timber is stronger along the grain and can be more difficult to cut or shape against the grain.
  • Surface Defects – Imperfections such as knots, cracks, and splits that can affect the wood's strength, appearance, and suitability for certain applications. These defects may reduce the overall value of the timber.
  • Warpage – The distortion or bending of timber due to changes in moisture content, temperature, or drying methods. Warped wood may be difficult to work with and may not maintain its shape in certain applications.
  • Shrinkage – The reduction in size of timber as it dries out and loses moisture. Shrinkage can cause timber to change shape, which must be considered in design and construction.
  • Splitting – The separation of the wood along its grain, typically caused by excessive dryness or stress. Splitting can affect the structural integrity of the wood and its appearance.
  • Joining – The ability to bond timber pieces together. Different timber types have different joining properties, and choosing the right adhesive or method is essential for a strong bond.
  • Forming – The process of shaping wood into specific forms using tools or machinery. Forming can involve cutting, bending, or molding the timber to meet the desired specifications.
  • Steam Bending – A method of bending timber by softening it with steam, allowing it to be shaped without cracking. This is commonly used in making curved furniture and architectural components.
  • Laminating – A process where multiple layers of wood are bonded together to create a stronger or more durable piece of timber. Laminated timber is often used in structural applications.
  • Machining Qualities – The ability of timber to be processed using tools such as saws, drills, or routers. Different wood types have varying machining qualities based on their hardness, grain pattern, and moisture content.
  • Resistance to Decay – The natural ability of timber to resist the effects of biological degradation, such as fungal or insect attacks. Some timbers are naturally resistant to decay, making them ideal for outdoor and high-moisture environments.
  • Moisture Resistance – The ability of timber to withstand exposure to moisture without warping, swelling, or rotting. Moisture-resistant timbers are often used in environments subject to high humidity or rain.
  • Toxicity – Some timber types, such as those treated with preservatives or naturally occurring chemicals, can be toxic to humans or animals. It's important to be aware of any risks related to toxicity, especially when using wood in construction or furniture that will be in contact with people.

General comparisons of Hardwoods and Softwoods

Using these general rules will help you answer most questions relating to Hardwoods and Softwoods:

  • Hardwoods tend to be more expensive due to the longer time to grow to maturity
  • Hardwoods tend to be denser due to slow growth times, this makes them more durable, stronger, harder and tougher than softwoods
  • Hardwoods tend to be darker than softwoods

Softwoods

Pine:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Easy to work
  • Straight grain
  • Affordable
  • Prone to knotting
  • Not very durable
  • Rots/corrodes easily

Uses: Roof joists, Floorboards, Construction, Furniture



Spruce:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Lightweight and easy to handle
  • Strong and flexible, making it ideal for structural uses
  • Good for sound insulation due to its resonance
  • Prone to splitting and cracking if not treated properly
  • Not as durable as other hardwoods
  • Susceptible to insect and fungal attacks without treatment

Uses: Construction (Structural Beams), Musical Instruments, Paper Production, Furniture





Douglas Fir:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Highly durable and resistant to decay
  • Strong, with a high strength-to-weight ratio
  • Great workability, especially for cutting and shaping
  • Can have a coarse texture, making it less smooth than other woods
  • Can be prone to splitting and knotting
  • Vulnerable to weathering if not treated or sealed properly

Uses: Construction (Timber Frames, Structural Components), Flooring, Furniture, Plywood





Redwood:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Aesthetically pleasing
  • Very durable
  • Doesn't warp
  • Easily dented
  • Expensive
  • Can have irregular grain

Uses: Furniture, Construction, Exterior furniture, Veneers



Cedar:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Doesn't warp easily
  • Easy to work
  • Naturally resistant to wear and decay
  • Prone to knotting
  • Susceptible to biological corrosion
  • Highly flammable unless treated

Uses: Garden sheds, Decking, Floorboards



Larch:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Aesthetically pleasing
  • Easy to work
  • Good availability
  • Natural resins can damage tools
  • Prone to knotting/warping
  • Difficult to treat

Uses: Furniture, Cladding, Boatbuilding, Veneers



Hardwoods

Oak:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Strong
  • Works Well
  • Durable
  • Expensive
  • Heavy
  • Prone to Splitting
  • Can be Physically Hard

Uses: Garden furniture, construction, High quality furniture



Ash:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Strong and tough, with excellent shock resistance
  • Flexible and durable, making it ideal for bending and shaping
  • Aesthetic, light colour and attractive grain pattern
  • Can be difficult to work with due to its density
  • Prone to splitting when dried improperly
  • Vulnerable to attack by insects and fungi if untreated

Uses: Furniture, Tool Handles, Sports Equipment (e.g., baseball bats), Flooring, Cabinetry





Mahogany:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Easy to work
  • Durable
  • Finishes well
  • Very few knots
  • Grain can be variable
  • Prone to warping
  • Prone to Splitting
  • Physical hardness varies

Uses: Furniture, Veneers, Floorboards



Teak:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Highly durable and resistant to moisture and decay
  • Beautiful golden-brown colour and attractive grain
  • Excellent resistance to insects, making it ideal for outdoor use
  • Expensive due to its rarity and high demand
  • Can be difficult to work with due to its hardness
  • Can darken over time with exposure to sunlight

Uses: Outdoor Furniture, Decking, Boat Building, Flooring, High-End Cabinetry





Birch:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Light in colour with a fine, smooth grain
  • Strong and hard, making it suitable for high-wear applications
  • Easy to work with, especially for turning and shaping
  • Prone to developing scratches and dents easily
  • Susceptible to warping or cracking if not dried properly
  • Can be difficult to stain evenly due to its porous nature

Uses: Furniture, Plywood, Veneer, Interior Trim, Cabinetry





Beech:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Physically hard
  • Tough
  • Polishes well
  • Can be prone to warping
  • Not suitable for outside applications
  • Can be difficult to work

Uses: Workshop benches, School desks, Furniture



General comparisons of Natural timbers and Manmade timbers

Using these general rules will help you answer most questions relating to Natural timbers and Manmade timbers:

  • Manmade boards are not limited by the size of the tree, they can be created in any size, shape or thickness
  • Manmade boards are often covered in a wood veneer for greater strength, durability and for aesthetics
  • Manmade boards are harder to recycle due to the adhesives in them
  • Manmade boards tend to be cheaper than natural timbers due to lower quality wood
  • Manmade boards don't have a grain (except Plywood) which makes them stable in all directions and easier to machine
  • Manmade boards are more readily available
  • Manmade boards are made from recycled timber so they are more sustainable

Manufactured Boards

Plywood:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Widely available
  • Cheap
  • Uniform strength
  • Easy to work with
  • Can be finished easily with varnishes etc
  • Only comes in certain thicknesses
  • Edges can splinter
  • Doesn't do well with moisture
  • Not as durable as natural timber

Uses: Beams, Interior structures, shipping crates



Medium Density Fibreboard (MDF):

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Widely available
  • Cheap
  • Doesn't warp due to lack of grain structure
  • No knots
  • Comes in any size/thickness
  • Not as durable as natural timber
  • Doesn't take nails/screws well
  • Doesn't do well with moisture
  • Poor surface finish without veneer

Uses: Roofing, Tables, Cabinets



Veneers and melamine formaldehyde laminates

Veneers and Melamine Formaldehyde Laminates:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Veneers: Offers a natural wood appearance at a fraction of the cost of solid wood
  • Veneers: Can be applied to a variety of substrates, enhancing the appearance of cheaper materials
  • Melamine Formaldehyde Laminates: Resistant to stains, scratches, and moisture
  • Melamine Formaldehyde Laminates: Available in a wide range of finishes and colours, including wood-like textures
  • Both: Cost-effective compared to solid wood, especially for large-scale furniture and surfaces
  • Veneers: Can be prone to damage (e.g., cracking or peeling) over time, especially if not maintained properly
  • Veneers: Requires careful handling during application to avoid damage
  • Melamine Formaldehyde Laminates: Can be difficult to repair if damaged, as the surface is not easily restored
  • Melamine Formaldehyde Laminates: May release formaldehyde fumes if not manufactured correctly, posing health risks

Uses: Furniture (e.g., Cabinetry, Desks), Interior Surfaces (e.g., Kitchen Counters), Flooring, Wall Paneling
