Materials can be enhanced to resist and work with forces and stresses to improve functionality

Why modify materials?

  • To improve strength, durability, or flexibility
  • To adapt materials for specific functions or forms
  • To reduce material waste and increase efficiency

Lamination (Woods):

Bonding layers of wood together for complex shapes or added strength

How it works:

    1. Thin layers of wood are cut out and stuck together using adhesive inbetween each layer (usually PVA)
    2. A former is used to achieve the desired shape
    3. Clamps/vaccuum bag apply pressure whilst the adhesive sets
    4. Once formed the laminated timber can be trimmed and sized

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Economic to use (uses whole tree)
  • Can be shaped into a curve/complex shapes
  • Comes in large sheets
  • Strong material
  • Lighter than solid wood
  • Poor surface finish
  • Layers of material are visable and not very aesthetically pleasing
  • Can be damaged by water/moisture which leads to delamination
  • Difficult to be recycled
  • Can release formaldehyde which is toxic

Uses: Bridges, construction, kitchen tables

Bending:

Using force or heat to bend materials into a desired shape

How it works:

  1. Material is heated (if needed) to become pliable
  2. A jig or former is used to guide the bend
  3. Material is held in place until set or cooled
  4. Can be used for strength or curved design

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Can create curved and strong forms
  • No need for joints or fixings
  • Improves rigidity of flat materials
  • Good for repeated production
  • Requires specialist tools or heating
  • Materials can crack if bent too far
  • Harder to repair or unbend

Uses: Chair backs, acrylic signs, metal casings

Folding:

Creasing material to form stronger edges or functional shapes

How it works:

  1. Material is scored, heated or creased along a line
  2. Fold is made to a specific angle (e.g. 90°)
  3. Fold can be permanent or temporary
  4. Used for structure, joints or compact storage

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Quick and simple technique
  • Improves strength of thin materials
  • No adhesives or fasteners needed
  • Useful in packaging and origami design
  • Some materials may tear or crack
  • Limited to certain shapes
  • Precision tools may be required

Uses: Cardboard boxes, sheet metal panels, display stands

Webbing:

Using woven strips to reinforce or carry loads

How it works:

  1. Webbing made from nylon, polyester or cotton
  2. Woven in a flat, strong structure
  3. Used to distribute load over a larger area
  4. Often sewn or looped into place

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Very strong and durable
  • Flexible and lightweight
  • Good resistance to wear and tension
  • Easy to attach or replace
  • Can fray if not sealed
  • Weakened by UV or chemicals over time
  • May absorb water depending on material

Uses: Backpacks, safety harnesses, tent straps

Fabric Interfacing:

Used in textiles to stiffen or support fabrics

How it works:

  1. Non-woven or woven interfacing is cut to size
  2. Fusible interfacing has glue backing activated by heat
  3. It is ironed onto the fabric to bond them together
  4. Used to reinforce specific areas like collars or cuffs

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Easy to apply with iron
  • Improves structure and durability
  • Can be cut to any shape or size
  • Lightweight and flexible options available
  • May peel off if not applied properly
  • Some types are not washable
  • May affect the drape of fabric

Uses: Collars, waistbands, structured bags, embroidery
