The modifcation of properties for specifc purposes

Paper and Boards: Moisture-proof Additives

How it's done:

  1. Coating Application: A thin layer of wax or plastic (like polyethylene) is applied to the paper surface.
  2. Lamination: For extra protection, foil layers are bonded to the board (e.g., juice cartons).
  3. Curing: The treated paper passes through heated rollers to set the moisture barrier.

Why it works: These coatings create a hydrophobic surface that repels water molecules.

Timber: Seasoning Process

How it's done (Air Seasoning):

  1. Stacking: Wood is stacked with spacers (stickers) between planks for air circulation.
  2. Covering: Top is covered to prevent rain damage while allowing side ventilation.
  3. Time: Left for 1-3 years (hardwoods take longer than softwoods).

Kiln Seasoning (Faster Alternative):

  1. Wood is placed in a heated chamber (50-90°C).
  2. Humidity is controlled and gradually reduced over 2-6 weeks.
  3. Moisture meters check progress (aim: 12-20% moisture content).

Why it works: Slow drying prevents internal stresses that cause warping.

Metals: Annealing Process

How it's done (for steel):

  1. Heating: Metal is heated to 700-900°C (cherry red) in a furnace.
  2. Soaking: Held at temperature for 1-2 hours per inch of thickness.
  3. Cooling: Furnace is turned off and allowed to cool slowly (may take 10+ hours).

Why it works: Heat removes internal stresses and reforms crystal structures.

Polymers: UV Stabilizers

How it's done:

  1. Additive Mixing: Carbon black or benzotriazole compounds are mixed into molten polymer.
  2. Surface Coating: For extra protection, a UV-resistant varnish may be applied.

Why it works: Additives absorb UV radiation before it can break polymer chains.

Textiles: Flame Retardants

How it's done:

  1. Chemical Bath: Fabric is soaked in ammonium phosphate or brominated solutions.
  2. Padding: Excess solution is squeezed out through rollers.
  3. Curing: Heated to 150-200°C to bond chemicals to fibers.

Why it works: Chemicals release water vapor when heated, cooling the fabric.

Electronics: PCB Photosensitivity & Anodizing

PCB Manufacturing:

  1. Copper board is coated with light-sensitive photoresist.
  2. UV light shines through a circuit pattern mask.
  3. Exposed areas harden; unexposed areas wash away.
  4. Acid etches away unprotected copper.

Aluminum Anodizing:

  1. Part is submerged in sulfuric acid electrolyte.
  2. Electric current (15-20V DC) oxidizes the surface.
  3. Dye can be added before sealing pores with boiling water.

Why it works: Anodizing grows a hard aluminum oxide layer (up to 0.1mm thick).
