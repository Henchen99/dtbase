Surface treatments and fnishes

The following page of notes will cover commerical processes for:

  • Papers and boards
  • Timber based materials
  • Metal based materials
  • Polymers
  • Textile based materials
  • Electrical and mechanical systems

Papers and boards

Printing:

Common Methods:

    1. Offset Lithography: Uses plates to transfer ink to a rubber blanket, then to substrate
    2. Digital Printing: Direct application of toner/inkjet without plates (ideal for short runs)
    3. Screen Printing: Ink pushed through mesh stencils (for thick inks/special effects)

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Wide range of colors and finishes (spot UV, foil stamping, etc.)
  • Scalable from small (digital) to massive (gravure) print runs
  • Compatible with recycled papers/boards
  • High precision with modern CMYK/Pantone matching
  • Can combine with coatings for durability
  • Setup costs high for offset/gravure
  • Ink absorption may cause slight paper warping
  • Limited resolution on textured boards
  • Environmental concerns with solvent-based inks
  • Long drying times for some methods (except digital)

Uses: Packaging (boxes, labels), books/magazines, stationery, retail displays, wallpaper, and security printing (e.g., tickets).

Embossing:

How it works:

    1. The paper is placed between the two formers
    2. The formers then close around the paper to form the shape
    3. Formers can be heated/cold

Advantages Disadvantages
  • High quality finish
  • Sophisticated appearance
  • Expensive

Uses: Toblerone card packaging, birthday cards

UV Varnishing:

How it works:

    1. A UV-reactive varnish is applied to the surface (by roller, spray, or curtain coating)
    2. The coated item passes under high-intensity UV lamps
    3. UV light triggers instant polymerization, curing the varnish in seconds
    4. No solvents evaporate - 100% of coating material remains on the product

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Instant curing (seconds vs hours for conventional varnishes)
  • High-gloss, scratch-resistant finishes
  • Excellent chemical and abrasion resistance
  • High initial equipment costs
  • Limited to flat or simple curved surfaces
  • UV lamps require significant energy
  • Potential yellowing over time with some formulations
  • Difficult to repair once cured

Uses: Magazine covers, packaging, luxury goods, furniture surfaces, and promotional materials where high-gloss effects are desired.

Timber based materials

Painting:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Stops corrosion
  • Aesthetically pleasing
  • Range of colours
  • Saves time/money
  • Chips/flakes away
  • Environmently unfriendly

Uses: Timber structures

Varnishing:

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Protects from dirt/sunlight/water
  • Enhances look of wood
  • Easy to apply
  • Can't see brush strokes
  • Can be dull colour
  • Bubbles can ruin finished look
  • Some varnishes are bad for the environment (brushes cleaned result in varnish into water system)

Uses: Hardwoods and sodtwoods

Tanalising (Pressure Treatment of Timber):

How it works:

    1. Timber is placed in a treatment vessel (retort) and vacuum-sealed
    2. Preservative solution (typically Tanalith E) is forced into the wood under high pressure
    3. The timber soaks for specified time (hours to days depending on thickness)
    4. Excess solution is drained and timber is left to dry/fixate

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Extends wood lifespan by 20+ years (resists rot, fungi and insects)
  • Penetrates deep into timber core (not just surface treatment)
  • Low maintenance compared to untreated wood
  • Cost-effective alternative to hardwoods
  • Can be painted/stained after drying
  • Contains copper/azoles (requires careful disposal)
  • Not suitable for indoor use (chemical fumes during curing)
  • May warp or crack during drying process
  • Cannot be used for wood chips/mulch (toxic to plants)
  • Requires specialist equipment for treatment

Uses: Fence posts, decking, garden furniture, playground equipment, marine timbers, and outdoor construction.

Metal based materials

Dip Coating:

How it works:

    1. The metal part is cleaned and pre-treated (degreased, etched, or primed)
    2. Submerged in a molten coating material (typically zinc, aluminum, or polymer)
    3. Withdrawn at controlled speed to ensure uniform coating thickness
    4. Excess material drains off and coating solidifies through cooling or curing

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Excellent corrosion protection (especially hot-dip galvanizing)
  • Complete coverage, including hard-to-reach areas
  • Durable, long-lasting finish
  • Can achieve thick protective layers
  • Good for high-volume production
  • High energy required to maintain molten baths
  • Limited to materials that can withstand molten temperatures
  • Dimensional changes possible with thick coatings
  • Surface finish may be less smooth than other methods
  • Not suitable for precision components

Uses: Structural steelwork, fencing, automotive parts, hardware, fasteners, and outdoor metal furniture.

Powder coating:

How it works:

    1. The metal is heated to 230 degrees
    2. The metal is then dipped into polymer powder
    3. The polymer coating is then allowed to cool

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Range of colour finishes
  • Non-toxic
  • Evenly coated finish
  • More protective than paint
  • Better for environment that paint
  • Harder to do than paint
  • Uses energy/needs energy for heating powder (unlike paint)

Uses: Cars, wheel arches, sports equipment

Galvanisation:

How it works:

    1. The metal is dipped into molten zinc
    2. The zinc acts as a sacrifical anode to protect metal underneath

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Prevents rusting/corrosion
  • Simple process
  • Quick process that can be done on a mass production scale
  • Zinc layer can be chipped away over time
  • Not a very aesthetic finish

Uses: Bridges, pipes, metal brackets

Polymers

Polishing:

How it works:

    1. Mechanical abrasion with progressively finer grits (120-3000+)
    2. Buffing with polishing compounds (typically polymer-based)
    3. Final gloss enhancement using rotary tools or automated systems
    4. Optional protective coating application (e.g., acrylic sealants)

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Creates mirror-like finishes on polymers
  • Removes surface defects and scratches
  • Enhances material transparency (for clear plastics)
  • Improves hygienic properties (reduced micro-pores)
  • Labor-intensive process
  • Heat generation may warp thin polymers
  • Surface degradation over time
  • Not suitable for textured surfaces

Uses: Acrylic displays, polycarbonate lenses, automotive trim, and medical devices.

Printing:

Methods:

    1. Screen Printing: Ideal for thick ink layers on 3D surfaces
    2. Pad Printing: For irregular polymer shapes
    3. UV Digital Printing: Direct-to-substrate with instant curing
    4. Thermal Transfer: Durable markings for industrial parts

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Excellent adhesion to polymer surfaces
  • Chemical-resistant inks available
  • Flexible for both flat and molded parts
  • High opacity on dark substrates
  • Surface pretreatment often required
  • Limited resolution compared to paper printing
  • Ink compatibility varies by polymer type

Uses: Product labels, control panels, packaging, and decorative surfaces.

Vinyl Decals:

Production Process:

    1. Design cut from colored vinyl sheets using CNC plotters
    2. Weeding (removal of excess material)
    3. Application of transfer tape
    4. Surface preparation and precise positioning
    5. Final burnishing for adhesion

Advantages Disadvantages
  • No drying/curing time required
  • Removable without residue (depending on vinyl type)
  • Weather-resistant formulations available
  • Cost-effective for short runs
  • Not suitable for textured surfaces
  • Limited durability compared to direct printing
  • Color matching challenges
  • Application skill required

Uses: Vehicle graphics, signage, equipment labeling, and decorative surfaces.

Textile based materials

Printing:

Common Methods:

    1. Screen Printing: Ink forced through mesh screens (best for bold designs)
    2. Digital Sublimation: Heat transfers dye into synthetic fibers
    3. Block Printing: Hand-carved blocks pressed onto fabric
    4. Rotary Printing: High-speed roller printing for mass production

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Vibrant color reproduction
  • Design flexibility (photographic details possible)
  • Good wash fastness when properly cured
  • Scalable from artisan to industrial production
  • Screen setup costs for small batches
  • Color limitations on natural fibers
  • Stiff hand feel with plastisol inks
  • Water consumption in traditional methods

Uses: Fashion apparel, home textiles, promotional wear, and technical fabrics.

Dyeing:

Process Types:

    1. Exhaust Dyeing: Fabric immersed in dye baths (batch process)
    2. Continuous Dyeing: For large volumes with padding mangles
    3. Yarn-Dyeing: Coloring fibers before weaving/knitting
    4. Garment Dyeing: Finished products dyed for vintage effects

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Penetrates fiber core for even coloration
  • Superior colorfastness compared to printing
  • Maintains fabric drape and hand feel
  • Efficient for solid colors
  • High water/energy consumption
  • Chemical wastewater concerns
  • Difficult color matching between batches
  • Limited pattern possibilities

Uses: Solid-color garments, upholstery fabrics, medical textiles, and military uniforms.

Stain Protection:

Treatment Methods:

    1. Fluoropolymer Coatings: (e.g., Teflon) creates molecular barrier
    2. Nano-Coatings: Silicone-based invisible protection
    3. Plasma Treatment: Surface modification for repellency
    4. Bio-Based Treatments: Eco-friendly wax/resin applications

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Spills bead up instead of absorbing
  • Reduces cleaning frequency
  • Extends fabric lifespan
  • Maintains breathability (unlike laminates)
  • Diminishes with washing (reapplication needed)
  • May alter fabric hand feel
  • PFAS chemicals in some formulations
  • Additional production cost

Uses: Performance apparel, restaurant linens, outdoor furniture, and automotive interiors.

Electrical and mechanical systems

PCB Lacquering (Conformal Coating):

Process:

    1. Cleaning: PCB is degreased and dried to remove contaminants
    2. Application: Coating applied via spraying, dipping, or selective dispensing
    3. Curing: UV, thermal, or moisture-cured depending on coating type
    4. Inspection: Thickness and coverage verified (50-150µm typical)

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Protects against moisture, dust, and chemical corrosion
  • Prevents dendritic growth and short circuits
  • Insulates high-voltage components
  • Available in removable formulations for repairs
  • Requires masking of connectors and test points
  • Thermal management considerations
  • VOC emissions in solvent-based types
  • Difficult rework on fully cured coatings

Coating Types: Acrylic (general purpose), Silicone (high temp), Urethane (chemical resistant), Parylene (vapor deposition)

Lubrication:

Methods:

    1. Oil Lubrication: Mineral/synthetic oils for high-speed systems
    2. Grease Application: Thickened oils for long-term protection
    3. Dry Lubricants: PTFE or graphite where liquids are unsuitable
    4. Automated Systems: Centralized oil mist or grease injection

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Reduces friction and wear by 90%+
  • Dissipates heat from moving parts
  • Prevents corrosion in mechanical systems
  • Extends component service life
  • Requires periodic reapplication
  • Attracts dust/debris (wet lubricants)
  • Potential contamination in clean environments
  • Temperature/viscosity relationship critical

Applications: Bearings, gears, sliding mechanisms, precision instruments, and high-vacuum systems
