Tools, equipment and processes

  • Wastage processes

Wastage processes

Die Cutting:

How it works:

    1. A sharp steel blade (die) is pressed into a sheet material
    2. The die cuts out shapes in one clean movement
    3. Often used with a press machine for speed and consistency
    4. Can include creasing rules for folding lines

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Fast and accurate for high-volume production
  • Can cut complex and repeatable shapes
  • Clean edges with minimal finishing required
  • Can combine cutting and creasing in one process
  • Dies must be custom-made – expensive upfront cost
  • Not cost-effective for low-volume production
  • Only works with sheet materials of limited thickness
  • Some material wastage from cut-off sections

Uses: Packaging nets, greetings cards, business cards, labels

Perforation:

How it works:

    1. Rows of small holes are punched into the material in a line
    2. This weakens the material along that line
    3. Allows the material to be easily torn or folded
    4. Often done using a rotary perforation blade or press

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Allows easy tearing or separation
  • Speeds up opening, folding or detaching parts
  • Good for tickets, packaging, and forms
  • Can be combined with other cutting processes
  • Reduces strength along the perforated line
  • Not suitable for all materials or applications
  • Holes may allow moisture/dirt through in packaging
  • May leave a rough or untidy edge

Uses: Raffle tickets, postage stamps, packaging tear strips, notepads

Turning:

How it works:

    1. Begin by placing a circular, square or rectangular shaped peice of metal/wood into the lathes drive area
    2. The metal/wood piece is typically secured using a pressure pad
    3. Once in place, the lathe is activated to rotate and press the mould against the metal/wood piece
    4. The rotational force of the lathe then deforms the metal/wood piece to achieve the same shape as the mould
    5. When metal/wood spinning is performed by hand, a worker manually presses the mould against metal/wood piece

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Complex designs/shapes can be made
  • Energy efficient
  • Good surface finish
  • Can be computer controlled
  • Dangerous pieces can be ejected from machine potentially causing harm
  • Some materials are very hard to turn

Uses: Table legs, table lamp, engine parts, handles

Note: Turning machines are different depending on the material being used.

Sawing:

How it works:

    1. A blade with teeth is moved back and forth (or in a continuous loop)
    2. The teeth remove material as they cut through it
    3. Can be done manually (e.g. hacksaw, coping saw) or with machines (e.g. bandsaw, circular saw)
    4. Used to cut materials into required shapes or sizes

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Simple and widely available method
  • Can cut a variety of materials (wood, metal, plastic)
  • Hand tools are inexpensive and easy to use
  • Machine sawing is fast and accurate
  • Creates material waste (kerf)
  • Edges may require finishing
  • Blades can wear out or break
  • Machine tools require space and maintenance

Uses: Cutting timber to size, removing excess material, shaping components

Vertical milling:

How it works:

    1. Material is clamped into place
    2. Cutter is selected and RPM is chosen
    3. The material always remains stationary while the machines cutting tool rotates
    4. As the cutting moves, it presses against the workpiece and shapes the material

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Visibility - can see machine work
  • Can be CNC controlled and so automated
  • Ease of use
  • Very precise
  • Uses electricity/needs to be plugged in
  • Expensive to buy machines

Uses: Cutting gears, produce slots, drilling

Horizontal milling:

How it works:

    1. Material is clamped into place
    2. Cutter is selected and RPM is chosen
    3. The material always remains stationary while the machines cutting tool rotates
    4. As the cutting moves, it presses against the workpiece and shapes the material

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Runs faster than vertical milling
  • Very precise
  • Can run at a higher capacity than vertical milling
  • Durable machine - can do many runs
  • No parting lines from mould
  • Not good at doing radial cuts
  • Machinery takes up lots of space compared to handheld version
  • Uses electrcity/needs to be plugged in
  • More expensive machinery than vertical milling

Uses: Cuttings gears, produce slots, drilling

Drilling:

How it works:

    1. Drill bit is selected and holes are marked up
    2. Drill bit is fixed in the chuck and tightened using a chuck key
    3. The table is set at a certain height and the RPM of the drill is chosen
    4. The hand wheel is lowered to drill the hole

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Quick
  • Low skilled
  • Cheap
  • Can be automated easily
  • Drill can overheat if drill bit isn't sharp or at correct rotational speed
  • Wood can be burnt
  • Hole finish can be poor

Uses: Drilling holes for products, seperating pop rivets

Cutting and Shearing:

How it works:

    1. Material is sliced or severed using a sharp blade or tool
    2. Shearing applies a high force to cut straight through sheet material
    3. Can be done manually with scissors/snips, or with machines like guillotines
    4. Often used to trim, resize, or shape flat materials

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Fast and efficient for straight cuts
  • Good for sheet materials like paper, card, and thin metal
  • Minimal dust or waste produced
  • Clean, accurate edges with machine shearing
  • Only suitable for straight or simple cuts
  • Edges may deform if tools are blunt
  • Not ideal for thick or complex materials
  • Manual tools require effort and can be inconsistent

Uses: Trimming sheet metal, cutting card or paper nets, resizing plastic sheets

Addition processes

How it works:

    1. The two materials being joined are cleaned before welding
    2. Then the two materials are held together using a former
    3. Flux is applied to prevent the join area from oxidation
    4. A welding torch then heat the join area and a brazing rod is used to fill the joint area
Advantages Disadvantages
  • A good general purpose joint
  • Cab be undertaken with little training
  • Low bond temperature needed
  • Can join dissimilar materials
  • Needs flux (if not weak joint)
  • Metal must be cleaned before hand (time consuming)
  • Not as strong as other welding techniques

Uses: General engineering, bicycle frame

    1. Clean the surfaces to be joined to remove any dirt or grease
    2. Position the two metal parts closely together (edge-to-edge, lap joint, etc.)
    3. Apply intense heat (often using an electric arc or gas flame) to melt the base metals
    4. Add filler metal (if needed) to strengthen the joint
    5. Allow the molten metal to cool and solidify, forming a strong permanent bond
Advantages Disadvantages
  • Very strong permanent joints
  • Can be used on thick materials
  • Good for structural and load-bearing applications
  • Suitable for mass production with automation
  • Requires skilled labour or training
  • High heat can distort materials
  • Special equipment and PPE needed
  • Not suitable for joining dissimilar materials

Uses: Car chassis, steel frames, shipbuilding, pipelines

Lamination (Woods):

Bonding layers of wood together for complex shapes or added strength

How it works:

    1. Thin layers of wood are cut out and stuck together using adhesive inbetween each layer (usually PVA)
    2. A former is used to achieve the desired shape
    3. Clamps/vaccuum bag apply pressure whilst the adhesive sets
    4. Once formed the laminated timber can be trimmed and sized

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Economic to use (uses whole tree)
  • Can be shaped into a curve/complex shapes
  • Comes in large sheets
  • Strong material
  • Lighter than solid wood
  • Poor surface finish
  • Layers of material are visable and not very aesthetically pleasing
  • Can be damaged by water/moisture which leads to delamination
  • Difficult to be recycled
  • Can release formaldehyde which is toxic

Uses: Bridges, construction, kitchen tables

Hard soldering:

How it works:

    1. The two metals being joined is cleaned
    2. Then the two materials are held together using a former
    3. The metal is then heated up to the same temperature as the melting point of the solder
    4. The solder is then applied to the joint area
Advantages Disadvantages
  • Stronger than soft soldering
  • Better aesthetics
  • Can solder larger objects
  • More skill
  • Higher melting point

Uses: Jewellery, ornaments, silverware, model engines

Soft soldering:

How it works:

    1. The two metals being joined is cleaned
    2. Then the two materials are held together using a former
    3. The metal is then heated up to the same temperature as the melting point of the solder
    4. The solder is then applied to the joint area
Advantages Disadvantages
  • Low melting point
  • Quick process
  • Little skill
  • Weak joint
  • Only suitable for small objects

Uses: Electronics

3D Printing:

How it works:

    1. A 3D model is created using CAD software
    2. The model is sliced into thin layers by slicing software
    3. The printer builds the object layer-by-layer using filament or resin
    4. Each layer fuses with the previous as the material cools or cures

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Enables complex and intricate designs
  • Low material waste
  • Good for prototyping and custom products
  • Can be automated with minimal supervision
  • Slow for large or solid items
  • Limited by material strength and type
  • Requires post-processing (e.g. supports removed)
  • Machines can be expensive

Uses: Prototypes, custom tools, low-volume products

Batik:

How it works:

    1. Wax is applied to fabric in a pattern or design
    2. The fabric is then dyed; waxed areas resist the dye
    3. Process can be repeated with different colours
    4. Finally, the wax is removed to reveal the full design

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Creates unique, decorative fabric designs
  • Traditional and artistic process
  • Can use multiple layers/colours
  • Inexpensive materials
  • Time-consuming process
  • Requires practice for precision
  • Hot wax can be hazardous
  • Not suited for mass production

Uses: Textile design, clothing, wall art, decorative fabric

Sewing:

How it works:

    1. Fabric pieces are aligned and pinned or clamped
    2. Needle and thread are used to stitch pieces together by hand or machine
    3. Different stitch types can be used depending on function or look
    4. Stitching holds materials securely in place

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Strong, flexible joins
  • Can be decorative as well as functional
  • Suitable for a wide range of fabrics
  • Allows customisation and repairs
  • Requires skill or practice
  • Manual sewing is slow
  • Stitching may weaken over time
  • Not suitable for rigid materials

Uses: Clothing, soft furnishings, fabric-based prototypes

Bonding:

How it works:

    1. Surfaces are cleaned and prepared
    2. Adhesive is applied between the parts
    3. Pressure may be applied to hold parts in place
    4. Adhesive cures (dries or sets), creating a permanent join

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Can join dissimilar materials (e.g. metal to plastic)
  • No need for heat or complex tools
  • Even distribution of stress across the joint
  • Good for smooth and lightweight components
  • Surface must be clean and dry
  • Some adhesives take time to cure
  • Not as strong as mechanical fasteners
  • Temperature or moisture can affect the bond

Uses: Plastic casings, model making, lightweight frames

Printing (Offset lithography):

How it works:

    1. Digital files are broken down by colour separation
    2. The image is etched onto an aluminium plate for each colour, through a laser
    3. Each image plate is then loaded onto a plate cylinder
    4. The plate cylinder will dampen the non-image area of each file with water
    5. A vegetable oil-based paint solution will then be added successively to the plate to imprint the colour
    6. The plate cylinder will transfer the colour to another cylinder which is equipped with a rubber blanket that will print it directly onto the paper

Advantages Disadvantages
  • High image quality
  • Suited to higher volume print runs of 1000 runs or more
  • Quick/easy production of printing plates
  • Printing plates last a long time
  • Expensive set-up costs
  • High running costs for small quantities

Uses: Books, business forms, magazines, posters

Deforming and reforming processes

Vacuum forming:

How it works:

    1. Create mould (air gaps, tapers, angles, rounded egdges)
    2. Place the mould on the platen and lower
    3. Clamps HIPS and heat until 'bouncy'
    4. Raise platen into plastic
    5. Vacuum pump air out
    6. Blowback a little bit of air to help release mould

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Ideal for batch production
  • Inexpensive
  • Easy to make mould/can be modified
  • Low temperatures which reduce energy use
  • Flexible manufacturing process
  • Moulds need to be accurate to avoid webbing
  • Large amounts of waste materials produced - bad for environment
  • Can only mould thin products which are weak/prone to cracking
  • Can only form open backed shapes
  • Cannot mould complex shapes

Uses: Yoghurt pots, blister packaging, inside of fridges

Creasing:

How it works:

    1. A creasing rule is pressed into the material using a die
    2. This compresses the fibres rather than cutting them
    3. Creates a controlled weak point to allow for accurate folding
    4. Often used with cutting dies in die-cutting processes

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Allows precise and clean folds
  • Speeds up folding in mass production
  • Reduces cracking or tearing along the fold
  • Often used in packaging and card products
  • Requires a creasing die to be made
  • Not suitable for very thick materials
  • May leave visible marks on surface
  • Less flexible than manual scoring for one-off work

Uses: Folding cartons, greeting cards, packaging sleeves, folders

Pressing:

How it works:

    1. Metal is rolled out and straightened
    2. Metal is cut to required blank size
    3. The blank metal is clamped over the die and held in the correct position using the back gauge
    4. A hydraulically operated punch is then pushed into the sheet metal
    5. Once the desired shape is formed the punch is retracted and the sheet component is ejected

Advantages Disadvantages
  • High strength in pressed parts
  • Can be mass produced
  • Expensive set up costs
  • Large runs are needed to be economical
  • Can make product heavy

Uses: Shaping metal, patterns on metal trolley bases

Drape Forming:

How it works:

    1. A sheet of thermoplastic is heated until flexible
    2. The softened sheet is manually draped over a former
    3. The plastic conforms to the shape as it cools
    4. Excess material is trimmed off

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Ideal for simple curved shapes
  • Low-cost and accessible process
  • Good for prototypes or small batches
  • Minimal equipment needed
  • Limited to gentle curves and simple forms
  • Labour-intensive and slow for large runs
  • Inconsistent results if not carefully controlled
  • Not suitable for complex or detailed shapes

Uses: Protective guards, screens, shop displays, packaging trays

Bending:

How it works:

    1. A material (often metal or thermoplastic) is clamped
    2. A force is applied to deform the material along a straight line
    3. The bend is held in place until the material sets (if heated)
    4. Can be done using hand tools, machines, or heat formers

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Quick and simple process
  • No material removed – minimal waste
  • Suitable for a wide range of materials
  • Good for both manual and automated production
  • Only allows straight-line bends
  • Sharp bends may cause stress fractures
  • Can deform surface or change thickness slightly
  • Requires accurate setup to maintain angles

Uses: Sheet metal fabrication, acrylic signage, enclosures, brackets

Folding:

How it works:

    1. The material is scored or creased along a line
    2. It is then bent to create a fold, typically at 90° or 180°
    3. Used for joining, strengthening, or creating 3D shapes
    4. Common in card and paper manufacturing

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Fast and easy process
  • Creates strong edges or joins
  • Useful for packaging and flat-pack products
  • Minimal waste created
  • Limited to certain materials (e.g. card, thin metal, plastics)
  • Creasing may weaken material at fold point
  • Less precise than mechanical joins
  • Requires alignment tools or guides for accuracy

Uses: Packaging design, folders, display stands, sheet metal work

Blow moulding:

How it works:

    1. Plastic granules fed through hopper
    2. Heated/melted along the Archimedes screw
    3. Extruded into hollow tube (parison)
    4. Tube is then clamped into metal mould
    5. Air is pumped in to inflate mould
    6. Mould/bottle is cooled to solidify plastic
    7. Product is ejected and trimmed

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Intricate shapes can be formed
  • Can produce hollow shapes
  • Ideal for mass production
  • High initial set up costs
  • Moulds are expensive to create

Uses: Plastic bottles, containers

Casting:

How it works:

    1. A mould is created in the shape of the final product
    2. A liquid material (such as molten metal, resin, or plaster) is poured into the mould
    3. The material is left to cool and solidify
    4. The mould is removed to reveal the final shape
    5. Finishing processes (e.g. trimming, sanding) are applied if needed

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Can create complex and detailed shapes
  • Suitable for mass production
  • Wide range of materials can be used
  • Good surface finish with minimal machining
  • Initial mould making can be time-consuming and expensive
  • Slow process compared to other methods
  • Some materials may shrink or warp as they cool
  • Health and safety risks from hot materials and fumes

Uses: Engine parts, jewellery, sculptures, tool components, plastic toys

Injection moulding:

How it works:

    1. Plastic granules fed through hopper
    2. Heated/melted along the Archimedes screw
    3. Plastic injected into the mould
    4. Two-part mould "negative" of the product
    5. Product rapidly cooled and ejected from mould

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Ideal for mass production
  • Low unit cost
  • Precise moulding
  • High quality finish
  • High initial set up costs
  • Moulds are expensive to create
  • Cannot create large mouldings

Uses: Casings for electric products, containers for storage/packaging

Extrusion:

How it works:

    1. Plastic granules fed through hopper
    2. Heated/melted along the Archimedes screw
    3. Plastic forced into the die
    4. The plastic is extruded 'pulled' throug the die and cooled
    5. Rollers pull plastic continuously
    6. The extruded product is cut to the desired lengths

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Low cost relative to other moulding processes
  • Uses thermoplastics which can be remoulded
  • Waste material can be reused
  • Plastic can be manipulated after extrusion before fully cooled (e.g. bends/curves)
  • Hard to predict die swell (expansion)
  • Can only manufacture certain products

Uses: Collapsible tubes, guttering, straws, gear blanks
